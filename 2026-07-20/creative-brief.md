## 0. Engagement Scope & Run Parameters

**Engagement classification: Revamp (evolution).** This is a live site with real traffic, a working phone number in the nav, and a brand promise already in circulation. Concepts must recognizably descend from the current site. A visitor who called ASOT last month should land on the new homepage and think "they cleaned the place up," never "did I dial the wrong company?"

Locked parameters for this run:

1. **Homepage-first.** Concept generation covers the homepage only. The §9 page map lists the full existing 7-page structure; shared pages get built out after a concept is selected.
2. **Evolution, not reinvention.** Each concept preserves: the ASOT shorthand plus full name, phone-first nav with clickable tel: link, the "one number to call" promise at every scroll depth, and a palette that descends from the live site's teal/orange/warm-neutral system.
3. **Three Variation Pillars,** framed as one conservative evolution, one texture-and-atmosphere push, one bolder structural rethink.
4. **Copy rules are fail-closed.** Zero em dashes anywhere. Zero banned words (delve, tapestry, embark, journey, holistic, empower, best self, safe space, in today's world, in a world where, actually, genuinely). No "It's not X, it's Y" constructions. Every claim paid for by a number, story, named tool, or concrete specific.
5. **Factual guardrails (voice guide §8) are binding:** 1 to 15 person offices; human support is Mon through Fri, 8am to 7pm CT, never 24/7 (only the AI voice line is 24/7); HIPAA-friendly, never HIPAA-compliant; month-to-month, no long-term contracts; Mac AND PC, Google Workspace AND Microsoft 365; canonical domain is atxtechservices.com.
6. **Tech target:** precompiled Tailwind v4, self-contained static concept output, phone-first nav preserved.

**Flags carried forward from the voice guide:**

- **Office size:** live copy says 1 to 15 consistently; an earlier brief input said 5 to 25. Until Tag rules otherwise, all concept copy uses 1 to 15. Do not invent a new range.
- **Palette provenance:** the brand-extraction file was never populated (template only). Palette ground truth is the live index.html Tailwind config: teal #2a9d8f, orange #e76f51, cream/sand, warm neutral scale. The run notes referenced a "navy/tech palette"; the closest true statement is that the live colors come from a palette family whose deep tone (#264653, a slate navy-teal) the site never adopted. This brief adds that deep tone to the pool as the natural evolution color rather than inventing an unrelated navy.
- **Email/domain:** live copy shows hello@austinsmallofficetech.com. Canonical domain is now atxtechservices.com (the old domain 301s to it). Verify which email appears in final copy before publishing; concepts should template the email as a variable, not hardcode the old domain as the answer.

## 1. Brand Core

ASOT is IT support for small Austin offices with 1 to 15 people: therapy practices, law offices, CPA firms, and the other professional shops that have no IT department and never wanted one. One local number to call, a person picks up, same-day response, month-to-month, Mac and PC.

ASOT is NOT a big MSP (expensive, impersonal, overkill), NOT a break/fix tech who vanishes after one job, and NOT a burden shifted onto whichever staff member got drafted as the "accidental IT person." It is the middle path between those three, and the site's job is to make that middle path feel obvious and calm. The copy IS the product demo: a brand that promises clear communication and no jargon cannot publish one sentence of jargon.

## 2. Brand Manifesto

Somewhere in Austin right now, an office manager is on hold with an internet provider while three clients sit in the waiting room. A practice owner is googling printer error codes between sessions. The youngest person at a law firm just got handed a dead laptop and a look that said "you're good with computers, right?"

We started ASOT because none of that is their job. Small offices run on email, computers, printers, and a handful of tools, and when one of those breaks, the whole day tilts. The fix has never been a ticket queue or a 40-page service agreement. The fix is a local number, a person who picks up, and someone who already knows your setup because they set it up.

We would rather have 50 clients who love us than 500 who barely know our name. So we keep the promise small enough to keep: we answer the phone, we show up, we say plainly what we do and what we don't, and we stay month to month so the only thing holding the relationship together is that it works.

> **Pull quote:** "We keep the promise small enough to keep: we answer the phone, we show up, and we stay."

**Tagline / brand signature line:** "One number to call. We pick up."

## 3. Brand Essence

- **Purpose:** Small offices are the backbone of Austin's economy, and they deserve tech support scaled to them. ASOT exists so that nobody in a 1 to 15 person office has to become the IT person against their will.
- **Mission:** ASOT handles the everyday technology of small Austin offices (email, computers, printers, vendors, security basics, and now AI tools) through one local number and an ongoing month-to-month relationship.
- **Vision:** A city where every small professional office has a tech person who knows their name, and where "call ASOT" is the whole IT plan.
- **Personality:**
  - **Steady.** Same number, same people, same-day response. Nothing about ASOT is dramatic, including the design.
  - **Plainspoken.** Answers first, explanations second, in words an office manager would use on the phone.
  - **Honest about limits.** States what it doesn't do (no 24/7 human support, no server rooms, no compliance audits) as flatly as what it does. A stated limit is a competence signal for owners who have been oversold before.
  - **Neighborly.** Local Austin team, named clients, real testimonials, physical verbs: show up, sit on hold, fix it.
  - **Quietly current.** Sells AI voice agents with the same flat tone it sells printer support, with a live demo instead of hype.
- **Archetype:** Everyman, with a Caregiver undertone. ASOT wins by being the dependable neighbor with tools, never the wizard or the hero. The site should feel like a well-run front desk, never a tech company's launch page.
- **Point of View:** The IT industry sells fear and complexity to people who need neither. Most small offices need roughly a dozen things to work: email, files, printers, Wi-Fi, backups, passwords. ASOT believes the shinier the technology, the flatter the language should get, and that trust is built by pairing every promise with its limit in the same breath.

## 4. Emotional Identity

**Steady. Neighborly. Plainspoken. Unhurried. Capable. Sunlit. Relieved.**

"Relieved" is the target end-state of the page: the feeling of handing a problem to someone who has handled it fifty times. "Sunlit" is the visual register: warm Austin daylight, never fluorescent server-room blue.

## 6. Visual Direction

The current site is competent and clean: Inter, teal/orange on warm cream, icon cards, generous whitespace. Its weakness is texture. It has no photography, no material quality, no sense of place; it could be an IT site in any city. The evolution keeps the cleanliness and adds the missing physical world.

**Aesthetic principles for all pillars:**

- **Warm daylight over cool glow.** The visual temperature of the site is morning light on a wooden desk, never blue LED. Cool teal is the working color; warm cream and sand are the air it sits in.
- **Material honesty.** Paper grain, subtle noise (SVG feTurbulence at low opacity), soft warm shadows. Surfaces should feel touchable, matching a brand whose verbs are physical (show up, fix, sit on hold). Trend incorporated: film grain / noise texture from the Texture & Materiality category, because it directly cures the "flat template" symptom.
- **Accent discipline.** Orange (#e76f51) is spent, not sprayed: one CTA, one highlight, one badge per viewport. Trend incorporated: accent color discipline from Color & Light.
- **The phone number is a design element, not a footer fact.** It appears in the nav at every scroll depth and at least once as typographic furniture in the body. This is the structural expression of "one number to call."
- **Motion that breathes, never performs.** Modest scroll-triggered reveals and micro-interactions on cards and the call rail. Trend incorporated: purposeful micro-interactions. Trends skipped deliberately: immersive 3D/WebGL, experimental navigation, kinetic typography at hero scale, retro nostalgia. All four conflict with Steady and Plainspoken; an office manager mid-crisis needs zero discovery-driven layouts.
- **Anti-trend compliance:** no stock-photo hero with text slapped on, no generic gradient washes, no three-identical-cards-in-a-row without hierarchy, no Space Grotesk.

## 6.5. Photo & Image Direction

### 6.5A. Overall Photo Direction

The photographic world of ASOT is the inside of a small Austin office on a good day: a therapy practice's waiting room with a plant and a lamp, a two-desk law office with case files and a decent coffee maker, a front desk where the printer is working. Light is natural and warm, morning through mid-afternoon, coming through real windows; shadows are soft; color grading leans warm cream and sage with the teal living in objects (a folder, a mug, a chair) rather than in a wash. Compositions are candid-adjacent and environmental: rooms, desks, hands on hardware, streets and storefronts of Austin. People, when they appear, are seen at working distance doing believable things, never posing at the camera.

What never appears: server racks, blinking rack LEDs, headset-wearing support agents, handshakes, glass towers, hoodie-in-the-dark hackers, blue circuit-board abstractions, and any staged team photo of people pointing at a monitor. These are the visual jargon the copy explicitly positions against. Also excluded: recognizable real-business signage other than supplied assets, and any fabricated "team" imagery implying staff who do not exist. Generated imagery depicts places and work, not fake ASOT employees.

**Client-supplied assets:** `existing-site/images/leonard-weiss.jpg` (Leonard Weiss, M.D., Psychiatry Austin) is the only real photo and stays the testimonial anchor, alongside `psychiatry-austin-logo.svg`. No other named-person imagery may be generated or implied.

## 8. Typography Candidates

All available on Google Fonts CDN. Candidates, not assignments; each pillar picks its own pairing.

**Headline candidates**

1. **Inter Tight** — the continuity choice. Reads as the current site grown up: same skeleton as the live Inter, tightened for display sizes. Feeling: steady, familiar, professionally maintained.
2. **Bricolage Grotesque** — a grotesque with visible handwork in the details. Warmth and slight character without losing legibility. Feeling: neighborly, crafted, a shop with a personality.
3. **Sora** — geometric, slightly technical, excellent at very large sizes. Feeling: capable and current; the right register for a pillar that leans into the AI/phone-system side of the brand.

**Body candidates**

1. **Inter** — the incumbent. Zero-risk continuity for body text; pairs with all three headline candidates.
2. **Public Sans** — a plainspoken workhorse with a civic, no-nonsense register. Pairs especially well under Bricolage Grotesque.
3. **Source Serif 4** — for testimonial and long-form story moments only, not full body. Gives the Leonard saga and the manifesto-adjacent copy an editorial, human texture. Pairs under Inter Tight or Bricolage.

**Accent / detail candidate**

- **IBM Plex Mono** — for small functional labels only (phone number furniture, hours, "Mon-Fri 8am to 7pm CT" chips). Suggests switchboards and receipts, not hacking. Best fit with Sora.

Natural pairings: Inter Tight + Inter (conservative), Bricolage Grotesque + Public Sans + Source Serif 4 accents (warm editorial), Sora + Inter + IBM Plex Mono details (structural/technical).

## 10. Design Constraints

All three pillars must honor every one of these:

1. **Must NOT feel like an IT company.** No server rooms, headsets, circuit boards, matrix glows, blue corporate gradients, or handshake stock energy, in imagery or in styling.
2. **Never let the phone number leave the viewport.** The tel: link lives in the nav at every scroll depth, desktop and mobile. Any concept where the number is findable but not visible fails.
3. **No urgency theater.** No countdowns, no "Don't wait," no exclamation points in CTAs, no popups. CTAs invite a fit conversation.
4. **Zero em dashes, zero banned words, no "It's not X, it's Y" sentences** in any copy the concept renders. Fail-closed: violating copy is rejected, not edited around.
5. **Never contradict the factual guardrails:** 1 to 15 people; human hours Mon through Fri 8am to 7pm CT; only the AI line is 24/7; HIPAA-friendly, never HIPAA-compliant; month-to-month; Mac AND PC; Google Workspace AND Microsoft 365.
6. **The honest-scope content stays prominent.** A redesign that hides "what we don't do" breaks the brand's trust mechanism.
7. **Avoid template tells:** no three-identical-cards default rhythm without hierarchy, no generic gradient hero, no stock-photo-with-overlay hero, no Space Grotesk.
8. **Testimonials are never fabricated or compressed.** Real photo only for Leonard; first name plus last initial for everyone else; long-form story shape preserved.
9. **Must recognizably descend from the live site.** Teal must survive in every pillar; the warm-neutral base may shift but never to cold gray or pure white-lab sterility.
